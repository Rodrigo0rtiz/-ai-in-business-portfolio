MIS 432  ·  Western Washington University  ·  College of Business and Economics  ·  Spring 2026

Over one quarter in MIS 432, I studied how seven real companies — from a drug discovery startup to Waymo's self-driving program — actually deploy AI at scale. In each lab I built a working analysis, dug into the underlying architecture, and stress-tested my assumptions against what the companies publicly reported. What I kept finding was the same gap: the AI itself is often the easy part. The hard part is the data pipeline, the governance model, and getting people to trust the output enough to act on it.

Key Takeaways
What I learned across seven labs
01

AI is only as good as its data pipeline. Every company I studied spent more engineering time on data quality, labeling, and freshness than on the model itself. The model is often the cheapest part.

02

Business value comes from workflow integration, not model accuracy. A 95% accurate model no one trusts or uses delivers zero value. Adoption, explainability, and human-in-the-loop design matter as much as benchmarks.

03

Every AI system embeds values, whether intentional or not. Uber's surge pricing, Airbnb's search ranking, and Spotify's recommendations all reflect tradeoff decisions that affect real people — and those decisions deserve governance frameworks, not just engineering solutions.

04

Agents that say "I don't know" are more valuable than agents that guess. The most important design decision in the Insurance Advocate lab wasn't the model — it was the instruction to disclose uncertainty rather than hallucinate a policy citation.
